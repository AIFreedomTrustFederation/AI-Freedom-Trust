# Sovereign Identity

Status: canonical doctrine draft  
Scope: identity model for Federation trusts, users, agents, repositories, and local-first applications

## Purpose

Identity in the AI Freedom Trust Federation is not merely a username.

Identity is the relationship between a steward, a trust, a local context, permissions, records, contributions, and voluntary Federation links.

## Core Principle

A person or trust should be able to exist locally before it exists publicly.

The system should not require unnecessary disclosure in order to create value, learn, build, document, or steward a project.

## Identity Layers

### Local Identity

Private identity stored locally by the trust.

Examples:

- legal name where needed;
- private records;
- local keys;
- documents;
- family or business records;
- private AI steward memory.

### Trust Identity

The operational identity of the trust.

Examples:

- trust name;
- mission;
- values;
- governance rules;
- stewardship roles;
- contribution history;
- public maturity labels.

### Public Federation Identity

The optional identity visible to the wider Federation.

Examples:

- public profile;
- public projects;
- public repositories;
- public contributions;
- public missions;
- public marketplace listings where permitted.

### Agent Identity

The declared role, permissions, and boundaries of an AI agent.

An agent identity must state what the agent may read, write, suggest, publish, or never do without human approval.

## Permissions

Identity requires permission boundaries.

Each trust should define:

- private files;
- shared files;
- public files;
- AI-readable files;
- AI-writable files;
- publishable files;
- sensitive files requiring human review.

## Keys and Credentials

The Federation should eventually support user-controlled keys, credentials, attestations, and recovery paths.

Until formal identity infrastructure is implemented, Markdown documents and manifests should clearly distinguish local identity claims from verified credentials.

## Reputation

Reputation should come from transparent contribution, not status games.

Examples:

- merged code;
- reviewed documentation;
- completed missions;
- verified services;
- published research;
- maintained projects;
- mentorship;
- stewardship;
- public goods.

Reputation must not become a substitute for consent, due process, privacy, or professional licensing.

## Identity Safety

The system must be careful with:

- legal names;
- addresses;
- precise location;
- medical information;
- financial information;
- family and custody information;
- private communications;
- biometric or government identity data.

## Design Goal

A trust should be able to say:

> This is who we are, this is what we steward, this is what we choose to share, and this is what remains local.
